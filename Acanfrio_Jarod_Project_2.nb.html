1. Introduction

When many people think of finance and trading, their heads fill with thoughts of Wall Street, major banks, and trading stocks and options among other things. But what if the largest financial market in the world wasnt stocks, options, or other products? In fact, the largest financial market in the world is FOREX, short for foreign exchange; the market where you can buy and sell currency. In 2019 alone more than $6.6 trillion was traded daily on the FOREX markets. (https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-09-16/global-currency-trading-surges-to-6-6-trillion-a-day-market) In this project I explore a model to predict percentage change in FOREX rates of the Yen-Dollar currency pair, based on several derived features from macroeconomic data.

2. Hypothesis

Starting upon this project, I thought to myself, What may indicate swings in exchange rates? After brief research four main classes of features were decided upon for forecasting exchange rate movements. I hypothesize that the following features will be significant in the regression for Yen-Dollar exchange rate movements.

  1. Metric of Comparative Market Growth
  2. Metric of Comparative Inflation
  3. Metric of Comparative Production Output
  4. Metric of Comparative Money Supply Changes

Metric of Comparative Market Growth

This feature represents the difference between the US stock market growth, and the Japanese stock market growth. I chose this because as markets grow, the currency the market is based on tends to strengthen in value. A feature relating these two countries growth of markets can help indicate which currency is growing stronger compared to the other, and may indicate movements in exchange rates.

Metric of Comparative Inflation

A common metric used to understand inflation in an economy is the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which measures the average cost of goods in a country. As the CPI percent increase over some time period rises we have inflation, and as it lowers we have deflation. Comparing the change in CPI of the United States and Japan will help us understand which economy is experiencing more inflation, which weakens the value of their currency relative to the other.

Metric of Comparative Production Output

Another useful metric for understanding how an economy is functioning is the Gross Domestic Product (GDP). The GDP measures the market value of produced goods in an economy; it is a strong indicator of economic health. Comparing the percentage change of each countries GDP we can infer which economy is growing relative to the other. The economy with greater growth should see its currency strengthen against the others.

Metric of Comparative Money Supply Changes

In an economy the money supply is the value of all currency in circulation. As an economic stimulus, governments will print and pump money into circulation; something we have seen recently in an attempt to stabilize the COVID-era economy. Rapid increases in the amount of money in circulation tend to lead to an increase in inflation. Looking at the Equation of Exchange we can see how money supply is linearly related to prices in an economy. We can directly relate how changes in money supply cause changes in price, a prime indicator of inflation. As the currency experiences inflation, it will weaken relative to the other currency. I compare the increase of money supply in each country to see which currency should experience more inflation; this metric is the last feature in my model.

Formal Statement of Hypothesis

\[ \begin{align*} H_0: &\left(\forall i \in [1,2,3,4]\right)\left(\beta_i = 0\right)\\ H_1: &\left(\exists i \in [1,2,3,4]\right)\left(\beta_i \ne 0\right)\\ \end{align*} \] My null hypothesis is that none of the features have a significant effect on the Yen-Dollar exchange rate, they all have coefficients equal to zero. My alternative hypothesis is that there exists some feature coefficient which is non-zero, indicating that the variable is significant for predicting the Yen-Dollar exchange rate.

Percentage changes in time-series have been shown to be stationary, it does not follow some underlying trend, making them a good choice for regression analysis. Using the percent change over the prior year also allows me to understand the trend in the feature; these values give us an idea of growth or shrinkage of the feature over the past year. I choose percent change over the year as opposed to other shorter time frames because it is long enough to capture a general trend, but not short enough to pick up a lot of noise in short term volatility.

# clean_columns(df)

clean_columns <- function(df){

  for(i in 2:ncol(df)){
    df[, i] <- dplyr::na_if(df[,i], ".")
  }
  
  # Convert df to a tibble of numeric columns
  df <- df %>% 
          tibble::as_tibble() %>% 
          mutate_all(type.convert) %>%
          mutate_if(is.character, as.numeric)
  
  # Return cleaned df
  df
}

Merge Multiple Data Frames

To merge all of the data frames in the project I wrote a function merge_df_set(). I decided to join all tables onto the Monthly table, as that is the sampling rate of our dependent variable in the study. I do not want to interpolate values of what I am trying to predict. It is also the sampling rate of the majority of the variables in the study.

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
# merge_df_set(df.list)
#
# Input: df.list
#   A list composed of cleaned data frames or tibbles
#   Assumes first data frame or tibble to join_left on is in index 1
#   Assumes first column in each data frame or tibble is DATE
#
# Output: df
#   A tibble of all variables merged together.
#
merge_df_set <- function(df.list){
    
    # Get our first data frame in the passed in list
    temp.df <- df.list[[1]]
    
    # For all remaining data frames, join them onto the existing data frame
    for(i in 2:(length(df.list))){
      
      # Join our variables on the date to align samples
      temp.df <- dplyr::left_join(temp.df, df.list[[i]], by = "DATE")
    }
    
    # Set output as merged data frame, and return
    df <- temp.df
    df
}

Interpolate NA Values in Data Frame

After merging our data frames, features that are sampled less frequently than the table we initially joined on will have Na values throughout the column. To eliminate these NA values, I use cubic spline interpolation to fill them in. Cubic spline interpolation was chosen over linear interpolation due to my assumption that the data at this point is non-linear. I cannot assume that it would follow a linear pattern to the next point; financial data is messy and complex - the linear assumption is too simple.

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
# interpolate_columns(df)
#
# Input: df
#     A data frame or tibble of numeric values with some NA's present. Using the 
#     zoo package I use cubic spline interpolation to convert the NA values to 
#     numeric estimates
#
# Output: df.return
#     A tibble of cleaned, interpolated numeric values
#
interpolate_columns <- function(df){
  
  # For all columns except DATE
  for(i in 2:ncol(df)){
    
    # Interpolate at a maximum of 100 continuous NA values
    df[,i] <- zoo::na.spline(df[,i], maxgap = 100, na.rm = FALSE)
  }
  
  # Return data frame
  df
}

Lagging the Financial Data

A main point of financial time-series modeling is to not only understand the relationship between several factors, but to also predict how a response will move based on a set of factors. In order to do this we need to lag our response by some number of periods, \(n\). Using the features recorded at time \(t\) to predict the response at time \(t\) doesnt help us predict; we already know the value of the response. Below is my function to lag the response on the features:

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
# lag_data(df, nper.lag)
#
# Input
#   df:
#     A data frame of values to be lagged a certain set of time periods
#
#   nper.lag:
#     The number of periods to lag the data by.
#
# Output
# 
#   df.lagged:
#     A data frame of lagged values
#
lag_data <- function(df, nper.lag, response){
  
  # Determine number of samples in data frame
  # Create empty tibble to fill with values
  # Declare vector to fill with lagged values
  n = nrow(df)
  df.lagged <- tibble::tibble(.rows = n - nper.lag)
  lagged.vec <- NULL

  for(i in 2:ncol(df)){
    
    # Get name of feature being lagged
    var.name <- names(df)[i]
    
    # If-else to determine how to lag variable
    # Response is lagged forward, features are lagged backward
    if(var.name == response){
      lagged.vec <- df[var.name][(1+nper.lag):n,]
    }
    else{
      lagged.vec <- df[var.name][1:(n-nper.lag),]
    }
    
    # Append lagged column to output data frame
    df.lagged <- df.lagged %>% tibble::add_column(lagged.vec)
  }
  
  # Return data frame
  df.lagged
}

Calculating the Model Features

As mentioned above in section 2, we need comparative features between the United States and Japan. Currently, we have features for each country, but they dont tell us how the two economys compare. To create the features that compare the US and Japan, I look at the spread in percentage change for each of the feature sets. For example, to compare the GDP growths I calculate the spread of US growth and Japanese growth. High values of this spread indicate that the US GDP grew more than Japans GDP, and vice-versa. This logic holds for all features. Below is the function I wrote to compute my final features for the model:

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
# calculate_features(df)
#
# Input: df
#     A data frame of variables to compute features from
#   
# Output: df.features
#     A data frame of computed features
#
calculate_features <- function(df){
 
  # Calculate GDP Spreads
  gdp_growth_spread <- df$GDPC1_PC1 - df$JPNRGDPEXP_PC1

  # Calculate Market Growth Spread
  market_growth_spread <- df$NASDAQCOM_PC1 - df$NIKKEI225_PC1

  # Calculate CPI Change Spread
  cpi_change_spread <- df$CPIAUCSL_PC1 - df$JPNCPIALLMINMEI_PC1

  # Calculate M1 Money Supply Change Spread
  m1_change_spread <- df$M1SL_PC1 - df$MANMM101JPM189S_PC1

  # JPY-USD Exchange Rate
  exchange_rate <- df$EXJPUS_PC1
  
  # Create output data frame of all computed features
  df.features <- tibble::tibble("exchange.rate" = exchange_rate,
                                "gdp.growth.spread" = gdp_growth_spread,
                                "market.growth.spread" = market_growth_spread,
                                "cpi.growth.spread" = cpi_change_spread,
                                "m1.growth.spread" = m1_change_spread)
  
  # Return data frame of features
  df.features
}

Correlation Results and How They Relate to Financial Theory

At this point I compare the correlation results between the features and the response and see how they align to financial theory.

Interpreting Exchange Rate Change Over the Prior Year

Lets consider the following example:

  • Exchange Rate in Year 1: 100 Yen to 1 US Dollar
  • Exchange Rate in Year 2: 90 Yen to 1 US Dollar

The percent change over one year is: \[ \Delta_{1Y}EXRATE = \frac{EXRATE_2 - EXRATE_1}{EXRATE_1} = \frac{90-100}{100} = -0.1 \] That is, the exchange rate decreased by ten percent. One dollar went from equaling 100 Yen, to 90 Yen. We can interpret this as the Yen strengthened against the Dollar. If the conversion rate were 1:1, the Yen would be as powerful as the Dollar. In this study decreasing exchange rates indicate that the Yen is strengthening, and vice-versa.

Interpreting GDP Spread Correlation to Exchange Rate Change

We have defined our GDP spread as: \[ \Delta GDP = \Delta_{1Y}GDP_{US} -\Delta_{1Y}GDP_{JP} \] Positive values indicate that the United States had a higher increase in GDP than Japan. As a result of this economic growth, the US dollar in theory should strengthen. From the above example a strengthening US dollar relative to the Yen should cause a positive increase in the exchange rate over the prior year. Our correlation coefficient here is \(\rho = -0.1364602\). This indicates that as the US has a greater GDP than Japan, the Yen actually strengthens against the dollar. This does not make theoretical sense. I look at the plot of Exchange Rate Percentage Change over the past year vs.GDP spread to look for any trends or outliers.

Determining the Sample Size for Model Fitting

I use G-Power to calculate the size of the sample to fit the model to. I select the Linear Multiple Regression: Fixed Model, \(R^2\) Deviation From Zero test from the family of F tests. My input parameters and reasoning for what they are set to are as follows:

  • Effect Size: \(0.1\)
    • An effect size of \(0.1\) is between small and medium. I dont expect these features to explain all of the variance in the exchange rates, but I do think they will contribute to some of it. Exchange rate movements are complex, being affected by social, economic, and political factors; we cannot reduce an exchange rate or its behavior down to a few macroeconomic variables. Using my three features is a gross simplification, but makes for an interesting study.
  • \(\alpha\): \(0.01\)
    • I chose a smaller probability of Type 1 error, because of the implications this model could have. Suppose the model proves very effective at predicting exchange rate movements. This model could be incorporated into some type of FOREX trading strategy. With real money on the line we would not want to reject the null in favor of the alternative; we would be sure to lose money.
  • Power: \(0.8\)
    • I chose a power of \(0.8\), because I think rejecting the null and being correct 80% of the time is enough. If we had strong theoretical evidence that the null was true but our experiment said otherwise, we could always re-test, or investigate further. Perhaps the sample has some extreme outliers and caused the output to be misleading.

As a result of the Power Analysis we see that we need 161 data points in our model fitting sample.

Test For Normality of Residuals

To test our assumption of Normally distributed errors, we can check our QQ-plot:

plot(exchange.rate.lm, which = 2)

We see that the standardized residuals follow the quantile-quantile plot closely through the range \([-2.5, 2.5]\). Past these bounds, the residuals deviate from the line a bit. We see that around the expected quantile of \(-2.5\) our residual is less than expected, being around \(-2\), and that it deviates briefly around the theoretical standardized value of \(2\), being closer to \(2.25\). For the most part our residuals are normally distributed, but further tests should be run to obtain a p-value in order to better say if the assumption is satisfied.

Test Assumption that Residuals Have Constant Variance

Again, we go to the Residual vs.Fitted plot to examine how the variance in errors changes, if at all, with an increase in the response.

plot(exchange.rate.lm, which = 1)

The residuals appear to have a tighter grouping with fitted values in the range \([-10, -5]\), but then the variance appears to stabilize for the remainder of the plot. There may some degree of heteroskedasticity in the model; to check further we run Whites test:

print(skedastic::white_lm(exchange.rate.lm))

According to our test, heteroskedasticity is present in the model and is significant at the level \(\alpha = 0.01\). The model violates our assumption of constant variance. Perhaps a variable transform could be used to fix this issue, but performing these transformations is outside the scope of this project.

Introduction of Measurement Error

I suspect there is some measurement error in the data, as I used interpolation to fill missing values when joining the data sampled quarterly onto the data frame sampled monthly. We used estimated values as two-thirds of the data for points sampled quarterly; for every quarterly sample two months between points were NA after the join. Clearly this introduces some form of error into the model.

Access to quality data was a limitation of this project. Unlike major funds and banks who pay thousands of dollars a year for high quality data from exchanges and other financial services companies, I used data from a public Federal Reserve database. I am tied to using data sampled at whatever rate they provide, and have to engineer it to the best of my ability to put it into a usable form. Had I had access to higher quality data I wouldve liked to perform this study on a weekly sampling rate.

7. Analysis and Conclusions

Running a full analysis of the Yen-Dollar Exchange Rate model, we see that all but the heteroskedasticity of the residual assumptions are met. I believe that using a variable transform somewhere in the model will resolve the issue of heteroskedasticity. The derived features for CPI and M1 spreads are in line with financial theory; the derived feature for the Market Growth spread was not intuitive in terms of correlation with the response which leads me to believe my understanding of its relation to exchange rate spreads is incomplete to some degree. In the model summary it is shown that all of our features are significant at the level of \(\alpha = 0.001\), yet we have not explained all of the variance in the exchange rate movements seeing as our \(R^2 = .2636\). Based on the significance of the features I reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative, but am hesitant in doing so as a result of the heteroskedasticity. Had additional assumptions failed, I would reject the significance of the model.

These results give me confidence that the selected features hold some explanatory power for predicting how the exchange rate will increase over the prior year two months out into the future. The \(R^2\) value is lower than I was hoping to see, but I am not entirely surprised. Financial markets are not the same as physical processes where certain features and values can be rigorously tied back to the response in question. Foreign exchange markets have many influences, and can be shocked at any time. This work was an attempt to simplify the phenomena of exchange rate movements down to a few simple spreads in macroeconomic variables of the countries with respect to the currency pair. I am very interested in exploring related work to this in the future.
